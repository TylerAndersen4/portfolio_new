01 Overview

Summary

I worked with a fictional client that wanted to move into the cloud storage and organization market. There were a few key features they knew they wanted, but I would need to back up these claims with data. It was up to me to refine this rough vision and begin developing a user-friendly cloud storage platform.

Problem

What features does the platform need to fit into the already crowded cloud storage and organization market?

Solution

For the final product, I designed a cloud storage platform that would allow users to create documents and spreadsheets, upload files, and share these files with friends and colleagues. The product is accessible on both desktop and mobile devices.

02 Competitive Analysis

Google Drive is easy to use and has many of the features the client was looking for (document creation, social sharing, file organization).

Pinterest showcases images nicely and is visual-first, but isn’t a productivity powerhouse like Google Drive.

Evernote has a modern visual design and is useful for organizing web links, but its UI can be overwhelming at times.

06 User Stories

After creating the personas and using the data from the survey, I was able to create the user stories. I organized these by role, task, and value, and then sorted them according to their priority level. For the MVP I decided to focus on account creation, document creation, document organization, and file uploading. This decision was based on user data that pointed to user’s most necessary features. Here is a snapshot of the high-priority stories for the "user" role:

08 Sketches & Wireframes

After the user flows I sketched out views for states within the product: landing page, sign up, sign in, forgot password, add content, create document, and upgrade account. I went through iterations of sketches while incorporating mentor feedback. I wanted to create a platform that was minimal but with the basic features a user would want.

Landing Page

Dashboard

User Testing Takeaways:

  • After this testing, I knew I would need to create a clear “Add” button. Users were unsure where to add new content.
  • I also found out that the flow for organizing a document was unclear. I would need to make it clear where users can organize a document once they’re inside.
  • Users expected confirmation pages after upgrading their plans, so I added a confirmation page to the mockups after.
  • I found out there was a confusing navigation on the left side of the dashboard. I cleaned up the navigation by removing redundant filters.

09 Logo & Branding

I used mind mapping and word association to come up with different images and feelings for the brand name. I settled on IdeaDesk, which I thought captured both the creativity and productivity associated with the platform I was designing. With the name in mind, I went on to create various forms of the logo:

Concept

I wanted to give a sense of cluttered papers on a desk, since these are the rough forms of ideas. I started by sketching different stacks of papers.

Refinements

I took the sketches into Figma and started playing around with different arrangements and shadows. I thought the primary font was powerful, so I wanted to keep the logo fairly simple. I experimented with placing the text in different areas, and liked how the papers overlapped with “Idea.”

Final Logo

For the final logo I changed the colors of the papers to make the design more inviting and playful. The papers on the left represent chaotic ideas, as if they’re coming out of a box. The final design is simple yet bold.

Color & Type

The IdeaDesk color palette focuses on white and greys with bright pops of color for buttons and accents. The palette brings a sense of fun and spontaneity to the brand. The brand typeface is Prata, a serif font that is balanced by the more modern Open Sans font for the body copy.

10 Mockups & Testing

I conducted two remote and one in-person test the desktop and mobile designs. During this round of testing, I wanted to get feedback but also hone my user testing skills. I worked on letting users have freedom in the product instead of directing them with verbal cues too much. This allowed me to get more insightful feedback that would shape future iterations of the product in important ways.

Product Pricing

  • User testing showed that the original pricing view was difficult to read.
  • In the final version, I added checkmarks for visual cues.
  • A strategic use of color focuses the user's attention.

Adding Files

  • Users said that having icons would make this dropdown easier to digest.
  • I learned that in general, designers should add visual cues in order to help the user.
  • In this case visual cues are also helpful for people who don't speak English as their first language.

Preference Testing

  • I used preference testing to decide between two views for adding files on mobile.
  • 90% of users preferred the native iOS view.
  • They mentioned that the native view reduces cognitive load because of its recognizable functionality.

11 Final Product

IdeaDesk solves the client’s problem by providing a practical and intuitive cloud storage platform. I relied heavily on user data and research during the design phases in order to deliver a successful product.

Dashboard

Sign Up

12 Conclusion

What did I learn?

  • Always save iterations of work for case studies.
  • Sketches and wireframes should be thorough and neat. These need to be detailed in order to clearly communicate with stakeholders early on and conduct successful initial testing.
  • Build desktop and mobile prototypes at the same time.

What are the next steps for IdeaDesk?

  • Build out the “social” view
  • Work on visual refinements
  • Build out a view for collaborating on a document
  • Create the profile settings page: remaining storage, avatar picture, etc.
